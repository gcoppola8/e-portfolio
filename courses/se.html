Discussion

My discussion in Unit 1

Software engineering is a young field if compared with other engineering branches, for this reason, many methodologies are still evolving and many studies are continuously challenged. 

In recent history, many important software projects didn't realize as planned and the researchers are trying to determine mathematical reasons why this happen as in Lehtinen et al. (2014).

As described, the root cause is not a single one, but it is a complex mix of a multitude of causes that can span from Tasks, People, Method and Environment.


In my opinion, the top 3 causes are:

- Incorrect project requirements: If requirements are incorrect, the project's aim is not clear then progress cannot be measured and they can lead to poor quality of the software implementation if any is produced.


- Poor planning: Before implementation given a satisfying set of requirements, a feasibility study should be done to estimate resources such as time, budget, people that are necessary to the completion of the project. Often companies try to cut corners minimizing cost assigning fewer people than necessary or not competent enough, possibly trying to spend less money on third-party software that could help the development process, cutting the legs to the project. Or poor planning could be the wrong estimation in time, this can be related as well to unclear requirements that only when started to be developed rise in complexity in an unexpected way.

- Poor software testing: In the end, software testing when done properly allows to measure the progress, monitor the quality, and then react accordingly all along with the duration of the project. Appropriate software testing can even compensate for poor technical developers.

Examples:

London stock exchange's TAURUS project costed 500 million GBP and in 1993 has been canceled. Initially, the project was supposed to last 6 months and should have allowed all parties involved in securities exchange to communicate without the use of paper to speed up the settlement process. As reported (Drummond, 1998) the project grow into an extremely complex one with unclear requirements and the main cause was that none of the stakeholders accepted compromises and as a result, the project management boiled together all their requirements.

United States Air Force designed the Expeditionary Combat Support System (ECSS) that should work as ERP to manage the institution's resources. With the intent to replace 200 existing legacy systems and cut expenses. The project was ongoing from 2005 until 2012 when USAF decided to interrupt the development after 1.1B USD spent and completion being still far away. Between the identified reasons for the failure, there is a lack of leadership and failure to reorganize the institution to properly adapt to the new system.
